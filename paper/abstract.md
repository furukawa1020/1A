# Abstract

Presenteeism support systems aim to help people who continue working or studying despite health-related difficulties. However, such systems may transform support into surveillance when they collect sensitive data, process it remotely, expose results to managers or teachers, or impose assertive labels on users' internal states. This paper introduces a surveillance transmutation model for presenteeism support and reports a 2 x 2 x 2 factorial study examining three design factors: processing location (cloud vs. local), third-party visibility (manager-visible vs. self-only), and output claim (assertive label vs. non-assertive cue). We measure monitoring feeling, label imposition, self-observation utility, willingness to use, willingness to disclose, and a design-audited exposure surface score. Our goal is not to detect presenteeism or diagnose mental states, but to identify design conditions under which self-observation support can reduce surveillance and labeling risks while preserving opportunities for personal reflection.
