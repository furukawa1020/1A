# Introduction

Presenteeism is not absence. People may continue working, studying, or participating in team activity while experiencing health-related difficulty, without leaving or formally disclosing that difficulty.

Support systems increasingly sense, infer, and visualize work-related well-being. In workplace and school contexts, however, support is embedded in power and evaluation relationships. A system that appears to help a person notice their own state may also create a record that others can interpret, evaluate, or reuse.

This paper argues that the privacy and security problem is not limited to inference accuracy or data protection after collection. Processing location, third-party visibility, and output claims shape whether a system remains self-observation support or becomes surveillance, assessment, or imposed labeling.

We introduce a surveillance transmutation model and test it through a 2 x 2 x 2 factorial scenario experiment. Our contribution is not a better detector of presenteeism. It is a security/privacy framing and empirical decomposition of surveillance risk factors in presenteeism support.
